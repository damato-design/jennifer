# Rethinking Promotions

**UX Case Study · Signet Jewelers**

---

## The Problem

![](/images/OH_Before.png)
Signet Jeweler's brand sites had a promotions problem... but it wasn't that they had too few offers. It was that the offers were everywhere and nowhere at once.

Banners repeated the same deal multiple times. Promotions appeared with no connection to what the user was actually shopping for. There wasn't a convenient place to discover what was available, and not an easy way to return to an offer you'd seen earlier. The experience felt very noisy.

This fragmentation led to user frustration and missed engagement. Not because there weren't good offers, but because the experience made it hard to find and evaluate.


## The Question

**How might we improve the promotions experience so customers see the right deals at the right time?**

---

## What we set out to build

A single, consistent destination for site-wide promotions; an **Offer Hub** that could handle two types of offers:

- **Discount offers** - price reductions (e.g., 25% off gold necklaces)
- **Non-discount offers** - value-added benefits (e.g., free shipping, loyalty rewards)

<b>The goal</b>: Centralize everything, integrate naturally into the shopping journey, and make offers easy to discover, understand, and return to without interrupting checkout. 

But how did we get there?

---

## Discovery

I didn't start with solutions. I started with questions. Alongside a dedicated researcher, we conducted a competitive audit of retailers then ran multiple rounds of moderated user testing across four concept variations and two refined iterations. 
![](/images/OH_Analysis.png)

![](/images/OH_Research.png)

Four clear themes emerged:

### Placement matters more than prominence

- Users consistently missed the bottom drawer CTA, with many failing the task entirely without a tooltip
- The side drawer was the most noticed and the preferred layout, but participants felt the CTA took up too much screen space
- Most users did not want to be taken to a new page to see offers, feeling it broke their shopping flow
**The recurring ask: make it accessible without making it intrusive**

### Too many offers erodes trust

- Most participants felt 4 to 6 offers was the right range — enough variety without feeling overwhelming
- Beyond that, perception shifted: too many promotions made the brand feel like it was struggling, or made individual offers feel less valuable
- Participants described seeing too many promotions as a signal that a brand "isn't doing well or is trying to get rid of inventory"
**When everything is a deal, nothing feels like one**

### Simplicity and clarity build confidence

- Users gravitated toward experiences that showed specific items, clear price differences, and straightforward value
- They did not want to compare, stack, or manually apply promo codes
**Show me the best price upfront, automatically**

###Personalization is a meaningful motivator

- Nearly all participants said they would log in or create an account in exchange for more relevant, personalized offers
- However, most missed existing sign-in prompts during testing entirely, signaling the prompt itself needed more visibility
**Personalization was seen as a reason to engage with the brand more, not just the offer**

**Main Takeaways**: Users want access to a range of offers, but on their own terms. The experience needs to be easy to find, easy to understand, and easy to trust, without interrupting why they came to the site in the first place.

---
## From insight to decision

Here's how each finding shaped the design:

### Placement: iterate on the side drawer, reduce the footprint
![](/images/OH_Process1.png)

Research confirmed the side drawer as the preferred format, but called for a less prominent, more collapsible entry point. The final design retained the side drawer layout while exploring ways to make the CTA feel less like it was competing with the shopping experience

### Number of offers: cap at 6, give power users a path forward

Testing pointed to 4 to 6 as the sweet spot, with 10 or more crossing into overwhelming territory. We capped the visible drawer at 6 offers with a clear "see all" option for deal-seekers who wanted to go deeper. 

This respected the casual browser without cutting off the intentional one

### Simplicity: lean into the implicit best price model

Signet's US brands already operate without promo codes, automatically applying the best price. Research reinforced this: users didn't want to hunt, compare, or do the math. We leaned into that by removing friction rather than adding explanation


### Personalization: design for it, ship without it
![](/images/OH_Process2.png)

The appetite was clear and consistent across participants, but constraints meant it had to wait.

## From Experiment to Evidence

We were taking promotions that lived in highly visible, prominent placements and moving them into a drawer. The team was nervous. The conventional assumption was straightforward: fewer clicks means worse performance.

What followed was two rounds of validations, one controlled and one real-world; both told the same story.

## What's next

This was an MVP. The foundation is built, here's where it goes from here:

- **Personalization for account holders** - the most-requested feature
- **Enhanced loyalty benefits** - deeper rewards for members
- **Promo code copy functionality** - a key addition for Signet's UK brands
- **Color exploration** - visual emphasis to surface priority offers
- **Integration with brand-owned offer pages** - consolidating the CMS so brand partners manage both surfaces in one place, reducing duplication and improving consistency
